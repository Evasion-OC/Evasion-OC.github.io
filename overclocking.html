❄️ Extreme overclocking

Extreme overclocking means pushing hardware beyond what air or liquid cooling can sustain — typically with single-stage phase change, dry ice, or liquid nitrogen (LN2). At sub-zero temperatures, silicon behaves differently: leakage drops, voltage headroom opens up, and the part can clock substantially higher than its retail rating. The trade-off is operating at the edge of what the silicon, the PCB, and your own patience can take.

Sessions involve preparing the board with dielectric grease and polyimide tape around the socket to manage condensation, mounting an insulated copper pot directly to the CPU heat spreader, and carefully pouring nitrogen to maintain target temperatures while sweeping voltages and clocks. A submission is usually a Windows boot, a benchmark run, and a screenshot — sometimes after hours of tuning to get the part to even POST cold.

  • Cooling: LN2 pots, dry ice, single-stage phase change.
  • Focus benches: 3DMark (Fire Strike family), SuperPi, wPrime, Cinebench.
  • Hardware: insulation, copper pots, premium PSUs, evaluation-tier motherboards.

💾 Memory overclocking

The most rewarding (and frustrating) sub-discipline. DDR overclocking is a balance of frequency, primary timings (tCL, tRCD, tRP, tRAS), secondaries & tertiaries, and voltage. A 200 MHz frequency bump means nothing if tRFC blows up; a tight tCL means nothing if the kit can't pass an hour of MemTest.

My tuning focus is the classic Samsung B-die on DDR4 platforms — still the sweet spot for chasing low CL at high frequency. Sessions involve methodical sweeps in MemTweakIt / Asrock Timing Configurator, validating with TM5 + Karhu RAM Test, and only then committing to a 3DMark or PiFast submission. DDR5 platforms are in the pipeline for upcoming sessions.

  • Kits: Samsung B-die (DDR4), DDR5 B-die / M-die (in progress).
  • Validation: TM5 1usmus_v3, Karhu RAM Test, HCI MemTest.
  • Tools: Asrock Timing Configurator, MemTweakIt, ZenTimings, ThaiPhoon Burner.
